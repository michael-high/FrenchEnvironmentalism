Sidonie-Gabrielle Colette (1873–1954)

Colette and the Place of Flora and Fauna in Modern Literature

Colette delved into a variety of artistic roles, employed many different aliases, and produced a rich, eclectic corpus of fictional and autobiographical accounts. Nature, however, served as a steady and fundamental presence throughout her turbulent life and unique career. Raised in the provincial region of Burgundy, Colette enjoyed a childhood of running free through gardens and fields, and she became famous for her fervent attachment to household pets. On her initial move to Paris, she noted plaintively “L’aversion mélancolique de ce que je nommais les maisons sans bêtes" (La Maison de Claudine).

Colette’s intense connection to place—whether her childhood countryside, her apartment in Paris, her summer villa on the Brittany coast, or her vacation home at Saint-Tropez—heightens her environmental sensibilities: “Je crois que la présence, en nombre, de l'être humain fatigue les plantes,” she observes in La Naissance du jour.1 She evokes a tender portrait of her mother (Sido), an animal and plant lover who will put off visiting her cherished daughter because she does not want to miss the moment when her cactus blooms (La Naissance du jour). Sido is accordingly a memoir that is grounded in the mother’s ‘natural habitat’ of the garden.

A contemporary of Proust, the sensory and psychological impact of childhood memories often sustains Colette’s prose; in one particularly Proustian scene, a conversation between Colette and Sido trails off into the recalled image and scents of chocolate and flowers: “je restai rêveusement suspendue à un parfum, à une image suscités: l'odeur du chocolat en briques molles, la fleur creuse éclose sous les pattes du chat errant” (La Maison de Claudine). However, in distinction to Proust’s dense prose, Colette’s economy of expression is reminiscent of Chateaubriand’s; similar to the nineteenth-century author, who also remains famous for his short stories and sensual prose (as well as his blurring of the line between autobiography and fiction), Colette’s narrator even questions language’s capacity to capture the experience of witnessing or remembering natural scenery: “Le reste vaut-il la peine que je le peigne, à l'aide de pauvres mots? Je n’aiderai personne à contempler ce qui s’attache de splendeur, dans mon souvenir, aux cordons rouges d’une vigne d’automne qui ruinait son propre poids, cramponnée, au cours de sa chute, à quelques bras de pin” (La Maison de Claudine).

If Colette is famous for these evocations of a lost childhood in the country, this should not mean that nature is a monolithic backdrop that serves as a therapeutic retreat. As Maurice Delcroix has noted, Colette’s representations of nature, which were often recalled during her life as a dancer and actor in Paris, depend on her development or role as a writer: “l’écriture de Colette est culture plus encore que nature” (58). Thus, aside from the thematic role of gardens, fields, and animals in her autobiographical accounts, there is a distinct ecopoetry to her texts that prevents any reductive readings of the presence of nature as mimetic or purely sentimental. In “Jour gris,” a story that appears in Les Vrilles de la vigne, the narrator’s aversion to a seascape produces a rêverie and a tantalizingly elastic prose that in fact vies with the insistent force of the surrounding waves and wind. The mysterious interlocutor further frustrates our desire to see the landscape that the narrator so urgently wants us to experience:

Ecoute encore, donne tes mains dans les miennes: si tu suivais, dans mon pays, un petit chemin que je connais, jaune et bordé de digitales d'un rôse brûlant, tu croirais gravir le sentier enchanté qui mène hors de la vie...Le chant bondissant des frelons fourrés de velours t'y entraîne et bat à tes oreilles comme le sang même de ton cœur, jusqu’à la forêt, là-haut, où finit le monde...C'est une forêt ancienne, oubliée des hommes, et toute pareille au paradis, écoute bien, car...

But any chance of getting lost in her childhood "paradise” is abruptly ended when she comes back to her senses:

Que t’ai-je dit? Ne le crois pas! Je t’ai parlé sans doute d’un pays de merveilles, où la saveur de l’air envire?...Ne le crois pas! N’y va pas: tu le chercherais en vain. Tu ne verrais qu’une campagne un peu triste, qu’assombrissent les forêts, un village paisible et pauvre, une vallée humide, une montagne bleuâtre et nue qui ne nourrit pas même les chèvres...

Nature is a seductive dream, and the writer knows it. Colette’s poetic prose captures the tragic yet somehow alluring sense of this awareness.
