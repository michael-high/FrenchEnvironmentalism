Baudelaire

Baudelaire and the "sublime deformation” of Nature

An almost lifelong Parisian, and one of the first theorists of the aesthetic potentials of the modern urban landscape, Baudelaire is not customarily associated with nature or environmental concerns. Throughout his influential collection of poems Les Fleurs du mal, Baudelaire overtly shuns romantic tropes of the reveries and purifying functions provided by the natural landscape:

Un soleil sans chaleur plane au-dessus six mois,

Et les six autres mois la nuit couvre la terre;

C'est un pays plus nu que la terre polaire

— Ni bêtes, ni ruisseaux, ni verdure, ni bois!

(“De profundis clamavi”, Les Fleurs du mal, 1857)


He also demonstrates how the modern artist has become impossibly alienated from the classical pastoral mode:

Le Poète aujourd'hui, quand il veut concevoir

Ces natives grandeurs, aux lieux où se font voir

La nudité de l'homme et celle de la femme,

Sent un froid ténébreux envelopper son âme

Devant ce noir tableau plein d'épouvantement.

Ô monstruosités pleurant leur vêtement!

Ô ridicules troncs! torses dignes des masques!

Ô pauvres corps tordus, maigres, ventrus ou flasques,

Que le dieu de l'Utile, implacable et serein,

Enfants, emmaillota dans ses langes d'airain!

Et vous, femmes, hélas! pâles comme des cierges,

Que ronge et que nourrit la débauche, et vous, vierges,

Du vice maternel traînant l'hérédité

Et toutes les hideurs de la fécondité!

(“J’aime le souvenir de ces époques nues...", Les Fleurs du mal, 1857)


As the poet’s stark contrast between classical (pantheist) and modern (Christian) epochs demonstrates, the Catholic tenet of original sin deeply informed Baudelaire's aesthetic. If women are “natural,” and thus reflect the ephemerality and ambivalence of nature, it is a result of their inherent evil. Makeup is not frilly artifice, but a necessary and even virtuous corrective:

La mode doit donc être considérée comme un symptôme du goût de l'idéal surnageant dans le cerveau humain au-dessus de tout ce que la vie naturelle y accumule de grossier, de terrestre et d’immonde, comme une déformation sublime de la nature, ou plutôt comme un essai permanent et successif de réformation de la nature. (“Éloge du maquillage,” Le Peintre de la vie moderne, 1861)


The enduring influence of Walter Benjamin’s writings, which highlight the material history of nineteenth-century Paris and the alienation of modern technology, have further distanced Baudelaire from the natural landscape. When invited to include poems to a collection celebrating the forest of Fontainebleau, Baudelaire, in his characteristic reveling in irony, famously responded by sending poems that are emphatically set in Paris:

Je ne croirai jamais que l’âme des Dieux habite dans les plantes, et quand même elle y habiterait, je m'en soucierais médiocrement, et considérerais la mienne comme d’un bien plus haut prix que celle des légumes sanctifiés. J’ai même toujours pensé qu’il y avait dans la Nature, florissante et rajeunie, quelque chose d’impudent et d’affligeant. (letter to Fernand Desnoyers, ca. 1853–1854)


Yet, the very irony embedded in this response is an invitation to question Baudelaire’s apparent scorn of the natural world. It is seldom pointed out that his most influential urban poems (“Le Cygne,” “Les Petites Vieilles,” and “Les Sept Vieillards”) were written in the Normandy port town of Honfleur, where the poet viewed both a maritime landscape as well as the plein-air pastel drawings by the up-and-coming Impressionist painter Eugène Boudin. The more that Paris and the phenomena spurred by rapid urbanization became the central component of Baudelaire’s aesthetic philosophy, the more nostalgia and a last gasp effort to encapsulate the natural world in aesthetic form infuses his essays and poems. While theorizing the modern urban landscape in the Salon of 1859, Baudelaire takes a curious yet significant look back to the romantic refrains of the first generation of French romantic authors: “Je regrette ces grands lacs qui représentent l'immobilité dans le désespoir, les immenses montagnes, escaliers de la planète vers le ciel...”.

By no means an environmentalist, Baudelaire nonetheless makes overt references to how pollution (whether atmospheric or acoustic) was part of the disorienting change that provoked his poetic imagination and formal experimentation. Rooted in the notion of sin (the word originally signified immoral stain), the pollution that was floating through Paris’s skies and seeping into its waters is a recurring component in Baudelaire’s grappling with modern life.

The poems featured here illustrate the tension between a modern, avant-garde art centered in Paris that is founded on a melancholic “souvenir” of the romantic potential afforded by the natural world. If mimetic Realist paintings in the manner of Courbet had caused a sensation in France, Baudelaire’s poem “Le Coucher du soleil romantique” captures the last rays of a romantic art that the modern artist can still grasp for inspiration. If prostitutes, laborers, the sick, and the alienated flood Paris’s streets, or if smokestacks release inky plumes above the urban landscape, the modern poet is there to capture the redemptive fleeting aspects of skies, clouds, rivers, and lush vegetation (“Le Crépuscule du matin," "Paysage"). In fact, Baudelaire’s later prose poems as well as his interest in pastels and watercolors suggest a turn to an art that seeks to capture the fluidity of impressions and natural phenomena. Just as Baudelaire defined the modern artist as stalking and sketching the phenomena of the modern city, Impressionist painters would follow suit with their rapid brushstrokes and their commitment to the representation of familiar landscapes and everyday life.

It is thus fitting that ”Les Nuages,” with its enigmatic ellipses capturing the effects of ephemeral clouds, opens Le Spleen de Paris, the collection of Baudelaire’s prose poems that his friends published shortly after his death: “J’aime les nuages...les nuages qui passent...là-bas...là-bas...les merveilleux nuages!". The atmosphere persistently beckons to even the most consummate modern urban poet.
