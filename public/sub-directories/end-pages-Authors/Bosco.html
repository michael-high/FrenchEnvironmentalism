Henri Bosco (1888–1976)

Provence and the Spirit of Place

Indelibly linked with his native Provence, Henri Bosco produced a wealth of prose works that examine the spiritual connections between human beings and the natural world. Privileging the child’s imagination, Bosco creates mesmerizing landscapes and magical creatures that heighten curiosity and create a reassuring sense of harmony in what was a decidedly bleak first half of the twentieth century. In L’enfant et la rivière, the apparently humble and mundane act of fishing forges a sacred kinship with the earth and its elements:

Or, les pouvoir secrets de cette nourriture donnent à celui qui la mange de miraculeuses facultés. Car elle unit sa vie à la nature. C'est pourquoi entre nous et les éléments naturels un merveilleux contact s’établit aussitôt. L'eau, la terre, le feu et l’air nous furent révélés. 1

It is perhaps this sense of escapism, along with a sizeable audience of children in his readership, that have tilted Bosco’s legacy more towards popular than “serious” literature.2 Nevertheless, the considerable body of secondary criticism, the many translations, the significant recognition and awards, and the various TV film adaptations that have resulted from Bosco’s overarching focus on the essential role of place in human experience make him an important point of reference in the canon of environmental literature. Having spent substantial parts of his career in both Algeria and Morocco, the North African landscape is also prominently featured in his prose works. Both lured to the Provençal landscape, Henri Bosco and fellow writer Albert Camus are both buried in the village of Lourmarin, situated at the foot of the Luberon Massif.
