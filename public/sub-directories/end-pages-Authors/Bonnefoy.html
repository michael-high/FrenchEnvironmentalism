Yves Bonnefoy (1923–2016)

“Where the natural opens itself to the human”

Yves Bonnefoy’s embracing of the formidably opaque relationship between humans and the natural world is woven throughout his first volume of poetry, Du mouvement et de l’immobilité de Douve, (1953). With Bonnefoy’s conscious departure from Surrealism, the natural world permeates his poems: “ce n’est pas d’une dimension d’imaginaire que Bonnefoy éprouve le besoin pour sauvegarder le feu nécessaire de la vie, mais d’une réalité simple, pleine, porteuse de sens – d’une terre, dira-t-il avec insistance” (Starobinski 12–13).

Yet, Bonnefoy's poems make no “romantic” or solipsistic return to nature. Instead, they express an intense exploration of being through which poetic language expresses the shadows and ever-present menace of death that haunt nature. The salamander, a creature that hovers between the frightened state of stillness and the terrorizing impulse of fleeting movement, symbolizes in Douve the anguish of a liminal state of being as natural phenomena impose their forces and impede the human capacity to know and to find meaning. But hope lies in language, which Bonnefoy describes as the mediator between the natural world and the human being: “At the intersection of natural reality […] and of language are found the garden, the earth, places of an ephemeral, haiku like presence where the natural opens itself to the human” (“Words, Names, Nature, Earth”). Bonnefoy’s continual explorations of the intersections of art and poetry present an ontological experience of nature rendered in lyric form.
