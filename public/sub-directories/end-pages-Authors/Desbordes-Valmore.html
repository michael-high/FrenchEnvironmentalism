Marceline Desbordes-Valmore (1785-1859)

Desbordes-Valmore’s Floral Ecofeminism

Despite the abundance of floral imagery and close interactions with the natural world that Marceline Desbordes-Valmore showcased throughout her corpus, there is scant secondary criticism devoted to a sustained analysis to the role of flora and fauna in her poems. Important scholarship has instead focused on the question of gender in her poetry, and deconstruct patriarchal readings and “idées reçues” that have stigmatized her lyric as “feminine.” Traditionally connected with a woman’s confined space (botany, gardens, embroidery, watercolors), a focus on flowers or female connections to nature would risk essentializing her lyric and relegating it to the category of minor works

Though Baudelaire is (in)famous for his misogynistic views on women and their disturbing portrayal in his essays and poems, he offers one of the more unique and sensitive readings of Desbordes-Valmore that highlights her attunement with landscape, atmosphere, and natural elements. Evoking what he describes as a heady calm before the storm—“Des massifs de fleurs y représentent les abondantes expressions du sentiment”—her poetry is a “simple English garden,” but a highly charged one that leads to a “lyric explosion.” The flood of tears that result from this cloudburst is a catharsis that leads to a poem, or “la fraîcheur et la solidité d’une nouvelle jeunesse.” The overflow of sentiment does not lead Desbordes-Valmore to familiar romantic clichés, but clears the way to vibrant expression with each and every poem.

Baudelaire’s admiration for Desbordes-Valmore invites us to consider the natural world in her poems, not as a marginal refuge for a female poet, but as a means to evoke a unique and powerful voice. For Yves Bonnefoy, Desbordes-Valmore's direct confrontations with nature distinguish her from the leading romantic poets of her day:

Romantique, si Marceline le fut, ce n’est guère qu'au sens où William Blake a pu l'être, ou un peu Wordsworth, mais qu’elle est différente de nos Hugo, Delacroix, Vigny qui eurent si peu le sens de la suffisance terrestre, l’un peuplant d’esprits la nature, l’autre la déclarant "insensible," et le peintre ne consentant d’y chercher que le dictionnaire de l’Idéal!

From a more distinctly environmental perspective, of particular note here are the floral poems that were written during her lengthy sojourn in Lyon, a famously contaminated city whose pollution Desbordes-Valmore decried on more than one occasion. Out of the effluence of the modern industrialized city grows her vibrant and sensual petals, which express the poet’s heightened desire and emphatic affirmation as a nineteenth-century poet. Though centered on women’s romantic fiction, Christie Margrave’s observation that novelists such as Mme de Staël, Sophie Cottin, and Adélaïde de Souza "remind the reader of pre-established women-nature connections, and then reveal the oppression of woman through that of nature” (47) prompts us to consider the seemingly innocuous sweet briar, the lightning bug, or the ephemeral mayfly in Desbordes-Valmore's poems as bold portraits of overlooked yet brilliant elements of nature that vie with the most sublime of vistas. If the critic Sainte-Beuve emphasized the naïve and instinctual aspects of her poems—“elle a chanté comme l'oiseau chante, comme la tourterelle gémit, sans autre science que l’émotion du cœur, sans autre note que la note naturelle” (1)— Desbordes-Valmore's form and imagery insist on her astute artistry.1

Aside from the flora and fauna poems featured here, also included is a poem in which Desbordes-Valmore’s narrator adopts the trilling chirping of a swallow, as well as one that resonates with Victor Hugo’s merging of the forces of history (monument) and nature (vegetation, birds’ nests) in his epic ode "À l'Arc de triomphe” (Les Voix intérieures, 1837). Like the swallow, the woman poet can easily, if surreptitiously, inhabit the imposing space of monuments.
