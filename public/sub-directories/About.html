French & Francophone Literary Ecologies of the Environment

About FFLEE

While the North American literary tradition abounds with nature writing and essays focused on conservation and land ethic, and boasts a vital history of ecopoetry, French art and literature is more famous for its legacy of “abstract” philosophic discourses (from Descartes to Derrida), its scrutiny of society and social mores (from Balzac to Beauvoir), and its intense focus on language and aesthetics (from Boileau to Bonnefoy). Nature as it is philosophized or represented in French texts seems to remain a decorative afterthought, or a brute force to surmount, or a backdrop that calls for the work of the artist’s imagination. With his characteristically vehement suspicion of Realism, the famously urban (and urbane) poet Charles Baudelaire leaves no room for nature itself within a landscape painting: “I would rather go to the theater and feast my eyes on the scenery, in which I find my dearest dreams treated with consummate skill and tragic concision. These things, because they are false, are infinitely closer to the truth, whereas the majority of our landscape painters are liars, precisely because they fail to lie.”1 Closer to our own time, the philosopher Luc Ferry’s Le Nouvel Ordre écologique (1992,) and its characterization of environmental philosophy and animal ethics as anti-humanist, holds persistent sway in contemporary French thought and culture.2

Yet, a flourishing corpus of scholarship produced just in the past decade in particular is a clear sign that what Stephanie Posthumus has theorized as a French écocritique—one that highlights “the specificities of and originalities of writing as producing a world rather than simply representing a world”3 —calls for a serious reassessment of the presence and role of nature in French and Francophone theory and literary works. She also reminds us that the purview of ecocriticism is not limited to natural environments: “French écocritique needs to examine a mix of representations of environments and landscapes in literary texts, from industrial and urban to mixed and rebuilt, from devasted and contaminated to flourishing and exuberant.”4 Thus, the title of this website project, French & Francophone Literary Ecologies of the Environment (FFLEE), embraces the diverse authors, landscapes, art forms, cultures, time periods, and eco-thinking that the study of French environmental literature encompasses.

While plenty of articles and chapters have been devoted to the fleshing out of ecocriticism, of French écocritique, and of ecopoetics, as well as to a number of disparate French and Francophone authors from all time periods and geographic areas, this website is designed to compile a comprehensive “go-to” index of authors along with an archive of readings that feature the primary texts themselves. Thus, this project is designed for both those researchers just making forays into French environmental literature, as well as for those who teach it and wish to expand their scholarship or syllabi. Each category (Ecopoetry, Flora, Fauna, etc.) contains a list of relevant authors and works, and each Author Entry contains a brief synopsis of the author and their relevance to écocritique, a list of works, and a list of further reading or links. The website primarily focuses on literature, but there is a small section devoted to painting. It is hoped that film will be incorporated in the future. Though it does not claim to be exhaustive, the general bibliography aims to be as comprehensive as possible, and will also be updated at regular intervals.

FFLEE, like French écocritique and the research it continues to inspire, is a work in progress. Author entries will be added and revisited routinely, and English translations will be indicated or added over time, and/or whenever possible.

In the spirit of collaboration that French écocritique has inspired, please feel free to contact me if you wish to make suggestions, corrections, or if you would even like to contribute an entry or a category of your own!

Karen Quandt

July 2021
